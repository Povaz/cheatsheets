1The problem: silent semantic drift

Silent semantic drift is the failure everything on this page exists to prevent: two stakeholders use the same word in good faith, agree they understand each other, and only discover at implementation or acceptance time that they meant different things. The smallest case: one term, one developer's reasonable interpretation, working code — that fails acceptance because the business meaning was different.

The drift almost always surfaces; the question is when:

Without a Dictionarysurfaces in QA, UAT, production, or the meeting where someone says "wait, that's not what I meant" — costs a sprint
vs
With a Dictionarythe disagreement is forced into the open during specification — costs a sentence

A Dictionary is therefore a shift-left intervention on vocabulary: pay for the disagreement at the cheapest possible moment.

2Ubiquitous Language

The idea's intellectual parent, coined by Eric Evans in Domain-Driven Design: Ubiquitous Language is "the practice of building up a common, rigorous language between developers and users" — one vocabulary, used in conversation, in specs, and in code, because "software doesn't cope well with ambiguity."

Rigor is a two-way contract:

  • Domain experts object to terms or structures that are "awkward or inadequate to convey domain understanding."
  • Developers watch for "ambiguity or inconsistency that will trip up design."

The payoff: no translation layer between business language and system language — the costly gap where meaning silently mutates is removed by not having the gap at all.

Ubiquitous Language

3Bounded Contexts

One shared language sounds like it should span the whole company. It can't — and DDD's "central pattern" is the admission: "total unification of the domain model for a large system will not be feasible or cost-effective" (Evans). The same word legitimately means different things in different parts of a business — "Customer" to Sales is not "Customer" to Support; both are correct in their context.

A Bounded Context is an explicit boundary inside which one model — one language — is consistent. Large domains are handled by "dividing them into different Bounded Contexts and being explicit about their interrelationships." The boundary is drawn by human culture: where the organisation's language changes, a context edge lives.

Tip The counterintuitive move: stop fighting polysemy, fence it. Trying to force one company-wide definition of "Account" produces a definition nobody actually uses. Two fenced definitions, each owned by its context, stay honest.

4From DDD to Context-Anchored Specifications

Context-Anchored Specifications (CAS) — this project's house framework — borrows exactly two ideas from DDD and deliberately nothing else:

DDD ideaBecomes in CAS
Ubiquitous Language (§2)The Dictionary
Bounded Contexts (§3)The Context
Language as a living artifactContinuous iteration + pruning at recurring reviews (§7)
Aggregates, Domain Events, Context-Map patterns, tactical design…Deliberately left out

The reason for the cut: CAS is a specification layer, not a code design methodology. It grafts onto an existing spec → user-story → acceptance-criteria pipeline and does one job — anchoring those artifacts to a shared vocabulary. One honest divergence, stated in the framework itself: a purist would object that a language not reaching the code isn't "ubiquitous"; CAS's position is that spec-level alignment is a meaningful win on its own, and code-level rollout is encouraged (carry terms into class names, endpoints, UI labels) but never enforced.

5The building blocks

Three definitions carry the whole framework:

Context· a vocabulary scope

A named scope with its own coherent vocabulary: a Title, a short description, a Dictionary, and optionally Relationships to other Contexts in plain prose. Not a code module, service boundary, or folder layout — two Contexts may live in the same file. The line is language coherence: one shared definition per word → one Context.

Dictionary entry· a (term, definition) pair

Written for humans, to remove ambiguity — not a glossary of every word, not an API reference. A term must earn its entry by at least one of: ambiguity (readers would interpret it differently, with concrete mistake potential), domain weight (business/legal/operational meaning beyond the everyday sense), or recurrence (appears across multiple Stories/AC; centralising prevents drift).

Anchoring· binding artifacts to vocabulary

A Story is anchored when tagged with at least one Context ([Contexts: …]) and its defined terms are backtick-highlighted. Acceptance Criteria anchor transitively — they inherit all of their parent Story's Contexts. A Story with no tag is unanchored and must be anchored before the next recurring review.

Watch out The inclusion bar is the framework's immune system. An ordinary English word with its everyday meaning does not get an entry — a bloated Dictionary dilutes the very signal that makes highlighting worth reading. When in doubt, leave it out and let the pruning pass (§7) confirm.

6The rules that make it mechanical

Most of CAS prefers judgment over rules. It is strict about exactly one mechanism — the pair of rules that turns highlighting from decoration into infrastructure:

Rule 1 — highlightevery use of a defined term, in its dictionary sense, is backticked in Stories and AC
→
definition changesa term's meaning shifts during iteration
→
Rule 9 — grepthe artifacts to re-review = exactly those whose text contains the backticked term
→
re-revieweach affected Story/AC is re-read and revised — or the change is reconsidered

Backticks are the chosen highlight because they render as monospace in every markdown viewer, survive format conversion as literal characters, and are mechanically greppable. The propagation contract is what justifies the highlighting overhead: without Rule 9, backticks are decoration; with it, the Dictionary becomes a navigable index of the spec.

The remaining rules in brief:

RuleIn one line
3 — at least one ContextEvery anchored Story carries ≥ 1 Context tag; most should have exactly one.
4 — transitive inheritanceAC inherit all of the parent Story's Contexts; no separate tags.
5 — inline disambiguationMulti-Context Story + term defined differently in two of them → `term[Context]` on that occurrence. Rare, and a reviewer's point of attention.
6 — split-or-keepMulti-Context Stories default to split if a single rephrase, independent terms, or a clean INVEST split applies; otherwise keep, with reasoning. Full rubric in §12.
8 — iteration is mandatoryA term that needs adjusting mid-Story/AC gets adjusted before the artifact is finalised.
10 — code usage encouragedCarry terms into class names, endpoints, UI labels — advised, never syntax-checked.

7Smells & pruning: keeping it lean

A smell means "this deserves attention" — not that the artifact is wrong (Rule 2). The framework supports shared understanding; it is not a compliance checklist. The catalog:

SmellWhy it deserves attention
Single-citation termMay not be recurrent enough to warrant an entry — the pruning pass decides.
Hard-to-define termIf nobody agrees on the wording, it's often two concepts wearing one word. Split.
Same word, two ContextsAllowed — but Stories spanning both will need inline disambiguation.
Multi-Context StoryOften legitimate; always re-check the split-or-keep rubric.
Circular definitionA defined via B, B via A — ground at least one in a non-Dictionary concept.
Definition > two sentencesUsually hiding two concepts. Split the term, not the paragraph.

Pruning is ongoing maintenance, not an event — it rides the team's existing review cadence (typically sprint review): zero citations → remove; one citation → keep only if judged important (important is deliberately undefined; favour lean); two or more → keep, but re-check for drift. There is no "freeze": releases and handoffs are management artifacts outside the framework.

Tip Citation counting is judgement-based today — grep by hand or ask; don't pretend to counts you didn't check. An automatic citation index is a documented future enhancement.

8The format: one section per term

The canonical Context block, as the contexts-dictionaries skill emits it:

# Context: Billing

The Billing Context covers invoicing, payment collection, and dunning.

## Relationships

Shares the term `Account` with the Account Management Context — different
concept entirely; the divergence is explained here.

## Dictionary

### Invoice

A formal request for payment issued against an Account. Once issued, immutable.

### Account

A financial ledger associated with a Customer, against which Invoices and
Payments are recorded.

Why sections and not a table — each term's heading is the term:

  • Maintainability — long markdown tables are painful to edit and review; a definition edit becomes a clean per-term git diff.
  • Anchors — every term gets a linkable heading (#invoice), so Stories, AC, and reviews can point at a definition directly.
  • Room without license — a definition that genuinely needs two sentences fits naturally; the two-sentence smell (§7) still polices the content.

Relative depth rule: a term's heading sits one level below its Dictionary heading — H3 in the canonical block above (Dictionary is H2), H2 in an HLDD per-Context document where the Dictionary is an H1 section (# 2. Dictionary). Same rule, different base depth; never mix depths inside one Dictionary.

9Dictionaries in a real project

A worked example — the HLDD of an online course platform, where the Lesson Attachments Context defines one term:

Attachment· Lesson Attachments Context

A single file uploaded against one Lesson, optionally paired with a free-text descriptive comment supplied at upload time. Bound to exactly one lesson and re-downloadable on demand for the life of that lesson. Filenames are unique within a lesson; the comment is fixed at upload — correcting an Attachment means deleting and re-uploading.

And the anchoring at work — the same term, backticked, in US-31's Acceptance Criterion:

docs/hldd/lesson-attachments/user-stories/us-attach-file-to-lesson.md · AC-31.1

Then "<file>" appears in that lesson's `Attachment` list,
    And the listed `Attachment` shows the comment "<comment>"

The three-skill division of labour:

contexts-dictionariesowns the Context blocks and every definition — nothing else touches them
→
user-storiestags Stories with [Contexts: …] and backticks their terms
→
acceptance-criteriahighlights terms in Gherkin; the .feature export strips them for the runner

Conventions in the example project: each Context lives in its own HLDD folder (docs/hldd/<context-slug>/) with the Dictionary as an H1 section and one H2 per term (§8's depth rule); a definition change triggers the Rule-9 grep across us-*.md files; and when a needed term is missing mid-draft, the drafting skill hands off to contexts-dictionaries and resumes after the entry lands — iteration, not a ticket.

10Three artifacts, three jobs

The framework produces three artifacts that are orthogonal — each prevents a distinct failure class, and none can substitute for another:

ArtifactQuestion it answersFailure it prevents
DictionaryWhat are we talking about?Semantic drift — two people, same word, different meanings.
User StoriesWho needs what, and why?Building the wrong thing — working code that nobody asked for.
Acceptance CriteriaHow must it behave?Building it incorrectly — the right thing, the wrong behaviour.
Tip Once vocabulary is aligned, a behavioural dispute is an AC issue — not a Dictionary failure. The orthogonality lets you route every spec disagreement to the artifact where it can actually be resolved.

11The 6-step process

The framework grafts onto any story + AC pipeline via six steps — the first four are sequential for a new spec, then steps 5 and 6 run continuously:

StepWhatDetail
1Spec FileAn unstructured natural-language document — what the thing does and why. Human-written, optionally AI-assisted. The starting point the framework grafts onto.
2Dictionary draftInitial Dictionary from the Spec File, before any Stories. Multiple Contexts if vocabularies clearly diverge. Deliberately small — important terms only.
3User StoriesConnextra + INVEST + anchor. Each Story tagged with Contexts; defined terms backticked. Multi-Context Stories run through Rule 6 (§12).
4Acceptance CriteriaBDD Gherkin per Story. Inherit parent's Contexts (Rule 4); use `term[Context]` only when same-term ambiguity surfaces at AC level.
5Iterative refinementAny earlier artifact may be revised at any step — no formal change-control gate. Iteration is the default mode.
6Recurring spec reviewAt the team's regular cadence (typically sprint review): prune Dictionary (§7); confirm anchoring (Rule 3); re-check multi-Context Stories (Rule 6); propagate definition changes (Rule 9).
Watch out AI-generated artifacts still need human review — especially for unhighlighted Dictionary terms, accidental synonyms, invented terminology, and cross-context vocabulary leakage.

Context-Anchored Specifications (framework document) § Process

12Rule 6 deep-dive: split or keep

When a Story spans multiple Contexts, the default is split — but only when one of three conditions applies. The rubric:

TestConditionAction
(a)Single-rephrase split — the Story can be cleanly stated using only one Context's vocabulary by removing or rephrasing one sentence.Split.
(b)Independent terms — cross-Context terms refer to independent entities that happen to appear together but aren't structurally entangled.Split.
(c)Clean INVEST split — both halves pass INVEST and yield two valid Stories.Split.
defaultNone of the above apply.Keep the Story multi-Context.
overrideExplicit reasoning required — recorded alongside the Story.Override the default (split when none apply, or keep when one does) only with stated reasons.
Tip Inline disambiguation (`term[Context]`) is needed only when the same term has divergent definitions across the Story's Contexts — rare. Most multi-Context Stories use plain backticks throughout.
