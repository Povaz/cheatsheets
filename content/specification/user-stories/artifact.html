1What a user story is — and isn't

User stories are "chunks of desired behavior of a software system" whose functionality "is seen as valuable by the customers or users of the software." Kent Beck introduced the term with Extreme Programming to keep requirements informal and conversational — brief enough for a note card, deliberately light on detail until development begins.

Three properties follow directly:

  • End-user perspective — a story names what a user wants and why. Never how: design and implementation stay out.
  • A unit of planning — small enough to schedule, prioritize, and discard independently.
  • Readable by a non-technical person — if only an engineer can parse it, it isn't a story.
Watch out Work with no user-visible outcome — "refactor the build pipeline", "automate DB restarts for QA" — is a Task, not a story. Forcing it into story form produces the traps of section 6. Track it honestly as a Task instead.

Fowler — UserStory (bliki)

2The 3Cs: Card, Conversation, Confirmation

Ron Jeffries' formula for what a story actually is — three elements, in time order:

Card"just enough text to identify the requirement, and to remind everyone what the story is"
→
Conversationthe requirement flows through "an exchange of thoughts, opinions, and feelings" — over time, at estimation, at planning
→
Confirmationthe customer defines "the acceptance tests that will be used to show that the story has been implemented correctly"

The load-bearing idea: the card is a promise for a conversation, not a container for the requirement. Detail deliberately lives in the Conversation; the Confirmation (acceptance tests) is what makes the light-weight card safe — "the confirmation … is what keeps the Circle of Life turning."

Tip This is why a story must never embed its Acceptance Criteria. A card that pre-answers the conversation has skipped the practice it exists for — the AC belong to the Confirmation step, written after the conversation (section 8).

Ron Jeffries — Essential XP, Card, Conversation, Confirmation

3The Connextra template

The standard shape of the Card. It originated at Connextra (UK, 2001) as the role–feature–reason format:

TEMPLATE

As a <who wants to accomplish something>,
I can <what they want to accomplish>,
so that <why they want to accomplish it>.

ClauseAnswersGuard
As a — the rolefor whom?A concrete role ("learner", "course admin"), not "user".
I can — the goalwhat?Observable behaviour the user can do, see, or get.
so that — the benefitwhy?The user/business value. Can't articulate it? Ask — don't invent.

The template works as "training wheels": it forces every story to name not just what the software does, but for whom and in pursuit of what objective. The same source carries a caveat worth keeping: the template is an aid, not a straitjacket — novice teams apply it too rigidly, when the detail is meant to emerge through conversation.

House convention This project writes I can instead of the original "I want" — a goal the user can achieve, not a wish they hold. Same three clauses, same semantics.

4INVEST: six attributes of a good story

Bill Wake's 2003 mnemonic — the standard quality lens for a drafted card. A good story is:

LetterQuestionWake's phrasing
IndependentCan it be built without first completing another story?stories shouldn't "overlap in concept" and should be schedulable "in any order"
NegotiablePhrased as a goal open to discussion, not a fixed implementation?"a good story captures the essence, not the details" — details are co-created during development
ValuableDelivers observable value to the named role?"it needs to be valuable to the customer"; split vertically through the layers
EstimableEnough clarity to ballpark the effort?"just enough to help the customer rank and schedule"
SmallCompletable within a sprint — ideally 1–2 days?"at most a few person-weeks" — smaller scope, sharper estimates
TestableOutcome observable enough to tell whether it was delivered?"I understand what I want well enough that I could write a test for it"
Tip INVEST is a lens, not a checklist to hide behind. A story can pass all six letters and still be wrong — if it feels off, trust that instinct and flag it. Note the bridge to section 8: Testable is the story-level seed of the Confirmation.

Bill Wake — INVEST in Good Stories, and SMART Tasks (XP123, 2003)

5Zones of control & influence

Gojko Adzic's systems-thinking frame — the sharpest tool for judging whether a story is real. A delivery team operates within three nested boundaries:

External environment

"elements over which we have no influence" — regulation, vendor roadmaps

Sphere of influence

"activities that we can impact to some degree, but can't exercise full control over" — user behaviour, business outcomes

the story's NEED lives here

Zone of control

"all those things in a system that we can change on our own" — code, schemas, configs

the story's DELIVERABLE lives here

The guideline: a healthy story's need ("so that…") sits in the Sphere of Influence, and its deliverable ("I can…") sits in the Zone of Control. When the need itself falls inside the Zone of Control, the story "is effectively a task without risk" — the team is writing itself tickets. That mismatch is the common root of the four traps in the next section.

Gojko Adzic — Zone of Control vs. Sphere of Influence (2014)

6The four traps

When a story breaks the zones pattern, it usually falls into one of four recognisable shapes. The rule for all four: warn, don't block — annotate with ⚠ and let the author decide. A false warning costs seconds; a missed one costs a bad story in the sprint.

TrapWhat's wrongExample
Fake StoryThe need sits inside the Zone of Control — an internal task dressed as a story."As a QA, I can have automated DB restarts, so that I can test faster."
Misleading Story (Solution Trap)The goal is a prescribed technical solution; the underlying problem is hidden."As an operator, I can run query optimization, so that reports are faster." — the real need may be "find data discrepancies quickly".
Dependency-LockedThe deliverable sits outside the Zone of Control (uncontracted vendor, cross-team API). Undeliverable in a sprint no matter how well written.Story requiring a third-party integration that isn't signed yet.
Micro-StorySliced so thin it carries no standalone business risk. Fine for short-term sequencing; regroup in longer plans."As a learner, I can click the login button, so that the click is recorded."
Watch out The Misleading Story is the sneakiest: it looks perfectly formed. The tell is a goal that names a mechanism ("run query optimization", "have a Redis cache") — ask what the user actually needs to accomplish, then let the team choose the mechanism.

Gojko Adzic — Zone of Control vs. Sphere of Influence (2014) (frame; trap catalog is this project's operationalization, after Gojko Adzic & David Evans — Fifty Quick Ideas to Improve your User Stories (Leanpub, 2014))

7Splitting with SPIDR

When a story fails INVEST-Small, split it. Mike Cohn distilled fifteen years of story-splitting into five techniques — he printed over a thousand split stories, grouped them on a wall, and kept the patterns that recurred:

AxisSplit by…Example
SpikeResearch first — "a research activity a team undertakes to learn more about some backlog item". Delivers knowledge, not the feature; use when the story fails INVEST-Estimable.Time-box "evaluate PDF rendering libraries" before the export story.
PathsWorkflow steps or decision branches."Pay by card" vs. "pay by Apple Pay"; Search → Add to cart → Checkout.
InterfacesUI surface or channel; simple interface before complex.Web before mobile; REST before webhook; read view before edit view.
Data"restricting the data that's supported" first, complex variants later.EUR accounts first, multi-currency later.
Rules"Temporarily relaxing support for the rules" — one business rule per story.Flat shipping first; weight-based rules as follow-ups.

Two extra axes worth keeping alongside SPIDR: CRUD (Create/Read/Update/Delete as separate stories when each carries its own value) and happy path vs. edge cases (core flow first; validation and recovery as follow-ups).

Tip Every split must still pass INVEST on its own — especially Valuable. A split that produces value-free fragments has manufactured Micro-Stories (section 6), not smaller stories.

Mike Cohn — SPIDR (Five Ways to Split User Stories)

8From story to executable spec

The story is where BDD begins. The 3Cs (section 2) map one-to-one onto BDD's three practices — the Card enters Discovery, the Conversation is Discovery, and the Confirmation becomes Formulation:

Cardthe user story — this page
→
Discoverytalk through concrete examples of the behaviour — the Conversation, structured
→
Formulationwrite the agreed examples as Acceptance Criteria in Gherkin — the Confirmation
→
Automationeach example runs as a test that guides implementation

Division of labour that follows: the story stays negotiable (INVEST-N) precisely because the Acceptance Criteria are written later, from the conversation — never embedded in the card. One story typically yields 1–3 Gherkin scenarios; more is a sign the story should split (section 7).

Tip Everything downstream of this handoff — Gherkin, declarative style, living documentation, the test pyramid — is the companion page: Executable Specifications with BDD.

9Stories in a real project

House conventions layered on the theory above — in a real project, a story like this one, from an online course platform's HLDD, shows them all in play:

US-31 · 🚧 Planned

Context: Lesson Attachments

Attach a file to a Lesson

As a Course Author,
I can attach a file to a lesson whose module is locked for review,
so that reviewers have the material they need.

  • US-X codes — every story carries an immutable, monotonic code. Never renumbered; removed stories retire their integer; splits get fresh codes. The code is what Acceptance Criteria inherit (AC-31.1 belongs to US-31).
  • Context anchoring — each story is tagged with its Context (per-Context folders under docs/hldd/), and Dictionary terms in the narrative are backticked (Lesson, Attachment) so definition changes are mechanically greppable.
  • INVEST as a deliverable — every drafted story ends with a per-principle pass/fail block, not a one-line "all good"; writing each line forces the reasoning into the open.
  • What Changed blocks — rewrites and splits carry a 2–5 bullet delta right next to the story, so reviewers see the edit and its rationale together.
  • Role concreteness — Course Author, Reviewer, Admin: the platform's defined roles, never "user".

House conventions — the docs/hldd/ folder layout; theory grounding per sections 1–8. Companion: Executable Specifications with BDD §9 for how US-31's AC become tests.

10Other smells

Beyond the four traps (section 6), six more smells surface during drafting and review. Each flags a story that looks finished but hides a problem:

SmellBad exampleFix
impl"add a /api/v1/users POST endpoint"Strip the verb-of-doing from the goal: "invite a new user, so they can start using the platform".
why?A story without a reason — no so that clauseIf the reason is "because the PO said so", reopen the conversation — the story is not ready.
role=user"As a user, I want…"Always prefer the most concrete role: learner, course admin, operator, finance controller.
mergeDescription contains Given…When…Then…Skipping the Confirmation layer — mixing two artifacts. Narrative on the card; AC in their own section.
capacity"we have 3 points left, slice off something tiny"Breeds Micro-Stories. Split along a real user-value axis (section 7), not to fit the burndown.
inventedRewrite mentions roles, screens, or integrations not in the inputLooks authoritative but silently expands scope. If the draft doesn't mention it, ask — don't guess.
Tip These smells are quick pre-flight checks: run through the list before calling a story "ready". Most are fixable by one round of conversation — a smell is a trigger to talk, not to reject.

Gojko Adzic & David Evans — Fifty Quick Ideas to Improve your User Stories (Leanpub, 2014) (operationalised)

11Choosing a splitting axis

When a story needs splitting (section 7), the first question is which axis. This decision guide routes from symptom to technique:

SymptomAxis
Story not estimable, real effort unknownSpike first
Multi-step user journeyPaths
Multiple channels or form factorsInterface
Clean data axis (region, currency, tier)Data
Many independent business rulesRules
Admin-style work over the same entityCRUD
Core flow clear, errors would double scopeHappy vs. edges
Tip When you split, return the resulting stories and a one-line note explaining the axis you used — so the reviewer can tell whether you picked the right one.

Mike Cohn — SPIDR (Five Ways to Split User Stories) (decision routing operationalised from the splitting axes)

12Collaborative refinement

Stories improve through structured conversation. Three practices dominate — each at a different altitude:

PracticeWhat happensWho attends
Three AmigosProduct + Dev + QA review the same feature from three viewpoints: business intent, technical feasibility, and testability.PO/BA + developer + tester (minimum).
Example Mapping~25-minute workshop refining the story and seeding AC. Four card colours: ■ story, ■ rules (AC), ■ examples, ■ questions. Ends with a shared map, or "not ready".PO + dev + tester; anyone can add a card.
Story MappingUser-journey × release-priority grid. Surfaces gaps, sequencing risks, and slice candidates at backlog level — not per-story.Product, dev leads, design; broader group for discovery.

The three practices are complementary: Story Mapping discovers the work at a high level, Three Amigos refines one story at a time, and Example Mapping drills into the AC seed. Use them in that order — or pick any one that fits the team's cadence.

John Ferguson Smart — Three Amigos requirements discovery; Cucumber Blog — Introducing Example Mapping; Jeff Patton & Associates — User Story Mapping

13Worked examples

Two fully decomposed stories — one healthy, one broken — showing how the concepts of this page combine in practice.

Happy path — Reset password via emailed link

WORKED EXAMPLE

Reset password via emailed link

As a learner,
I can request a password reset and receive a link by email to set a new password,
so that I can regain access to my account without contacting support.

INVESTVerdictNotes
Independent✓Requires only an email service — no story dependency.
Negotiable✓Goal is clear; implementation (link vs. OTP, email template) is open.
Valuable✓Removes a support burden; restores learner access.
Estimable✓Standard flow — team can size it.
Small✓One sprint, comfortably.
Testable✓Observable outcome: learner resets password and logs in.

Concrete role (learner), observable capability, clear value hypothesis. The 24-hour link expiry, email template, and rate-limiting rules belong in the AC — not in the story narrative.

Broken — Dependency-locked Klarna instalments

DEPENDENCY-LOCKED

Pay course fee with Klarna instalments

As a learner,
I can choose Klarna at checkout and split my course fee across instalments,
so that I can enrol without paying the full amount up front.

INVESTVerdictNotes
Independent✗Blocked on a signed Klarna contract and SDK access — both outside the Zone of Control.
Negotiable✓
Valuable✓Lowers the enrolment barrier — a real Sphere-of-Influence outcome.
Estimable✗Team cannot estimate until SDK access exists.
Small✓
Testable✓
Watch out Dependency-locked stories sit in In Progress until the external unblocks — that is not agile, it is a blocked ticket with the wrong status. Fix: split. Keep the in-team work as a "third-party instalment provider" abstraction at checkout; track the Klarna contract and integration as a separate dependency.
